JamLand / Performance Reality

What you are looking at is a prototype. It does not yet know what it is.

It processes audio through a configurable signal chain — pitch, delay, a test tone, a microphone, a canvas where parameters blend in space. These are scaffolding. The instrument underneath is something older and larger, something called JamLand, which is itself part of something older still: Performance Reality.

Performance Reality is not a product. It is a framework for a question that has occupied Christopher Overstreet for roughly thirty years: what does it mean to make music together, expressively, with technology, without reducing the act to something primitive?

The answer keeps changing. The question does not.

On Instruments

Miller Puckette — composer, mathematician, author of Pure Data — once described a real instrument as something you can pick up for the first time and have fun with, that shows you immediately how far you are from your own potential, and that makes you want to practice. Not a theremin drawn on glass. Not pitch and volume pretending to be expression.

The history of expressive technology is full of people trying to get past that narrowness. In 1725, Louis-Bertrand Castel described an ocular harpsichord that would project color as it played — music made visible. In the 1890s, Alexander Wallace Rimington built a Colour Organ and performed with it in public. In the 1920s, Mary Hallock-Greenewalt — concert pianist, inventor, litigant — built the Nourathar, a performance instrument for controlling light, and filed eleven patents defending her right to its principles. She sued companies. She won. She was largely forgotten.

Pablo Picasso drew with light in front of Harold Klarman's camera in 1949. Thomas Wilfred spent forty years composing silent Lumia — moving, colored light as autonomous art. Scriabin imagined his unfinished Mysterium as a total sensory event, multi-day, requiem for a civilization, with light and perfume and dissolution.

None of these people were making gadgets. They were asking what art could be if the boundary between sense and sense, between instrument and composer, between performer and audience, was permeable.

JamLand is asking the same question. The 2D interpolation space you see in the prototype — presets as points in a navigable field, parameters blending as you move — is a small, early version of something that wants to be a world: navigable in three dimensions, in time, by multiple people simultaneously, through gesture, through breath, through the position of a hand in a room.

The relationship between humans and their tools has always been more intimate than people admit. Notation did not merely record music — it changed what music was possible. Recording technology did not merely capture performance — it created new performance. The internet did not merely distribute ideas — it restructured which ideas could exist at scale.

AI is doing something similar, and what it is doing is not yet clear. What seems clear is that the most interesting things will come from people who approach it with curiosity rather than fear or uncritical enthusiasm — who ask it to say something, watch what it says, and then decide what it means.

That is what you are looking at. A prototype. A conversation that is also an instrument. A question about what art can be when the person making it allows the tools to participate.

JamLand — A Partial Description

Imagine a three-dimensional space. Points in that space are saved states — particular configurations of sound, light, parameter. Each point has weight: a sphere of influence. When you move through the space, what you hear is a weighted blend of the nearby states. Slow movement: gradual morphing. Fast: abrupt shift. Teleportation: instant jump.

The space can be navigated in first person, third person. The points can move over time, following trajectories. You can travel backward in time through the space. You can share it with other people.

This is not a new idea — David Wessel and Ali Momeni were working on interpolation spaces at CNMAT in Berkeley in 2003. What Christopher adds is the world: the space as a place, navigable like a game, inhabited by parameters that have names and weights and histories, traversable by multiple performers simultaneously in ways that are composed and improvised at the same time.

The instrument you are using now is the beginning of that space. The 2D canvas at the bottom of the page is the first room.
